City center & a little further out

Eat, wander, and drink like you live here.

Verona rewards a slower pace. The postcard sights are worth it, but the best meals and quietest corners are usually one street back from where everyone else is standing. Four lists, organized so you can find what you're after.

The essentials

Yes, everyone goes here — and yes, they're worth it. These are Verona's must-sees, the ones you'll recognize before you've even landed.

Verona Arena

A first-century Roman amphitheater that still hosts opera under the stars every summer — one of the best-preserved in the world. Even without a ticket, walking the same stone tiers as ancient Veronese crowds is worth the detour.

Piazza Bra

Casa di Giulietta

The (almost certainly fictional) balcony that launched a thousand photos. Shakespeare never set foot in Verona, and the house wasn't linked to the Capulets until the 20th century — but the courtyard, covered in love notes, is a genuinely fun stop.

Via Cappello
View on map

Castelvecchio & Ponte Scaligero

A red-brick medieval fortress turned museum, with its own crenellated bridge stretching across the Adige. Skip the entrance queue and just walk the bridge at golden hour — one of the prettiest views in the city, free of charge.

Corso Castelvecchio
View on map

Piazza delle Erbe

The old Roman forum, now Verona's liveliest square — market stalls by day, aperitivo crowds by evening, all wrapped in frescoed facades. Come once for the sightseeing, come back for the people-watching.

City center
View on map

Torre dei Lamberti

Climb (or take the lift, no judgment) 84 meters above Piazza delle Erbe for the best rooftop view in Verona — pink terracotta roofs in every direction, the Adige curling past, and the Alps on a clear day.

84m up

Ponte Pietra

Verona's oldest bridge — Roman-built, partially rebuilt after WWII with the original stones fished back out of the river. Cross it at sunset for the classic postcard view back toward the historic center.

Verona Cathedral

A Romanesque-Gothic cathedral with a Titian altarpiece tucked inside — quieter and less crowded than the Arena or Juliet's House, and a nice five-minute stop if you're already walking the river.

Off the well-trodden path

The spots locals actually go — some tucked into the historic center, others a short walk across the river or just outside the walls. Worth the extra ten minutes.

Giardino Giusti

A 16th-century Renaissance garden behind an unmarked door in Veronetta. Follow the cypress allée to the back and climb the narrow stone stairway inside the hillside tower for a secret, mostly-empty view over the entire city.

Veronetta · outskirts

Castel San Pietro & Teatro Romano

Ride the little funicular up from the Roman theater — still used for summer performances — to a hilltop fortress with arguably a better skyline view than Torre dei Lamberti, and a fraction of the crowd.

Veronetta · outskirts

Basilica di San Zeno Maggiore

One of Italy's finest Romanesque churches, with a bronze door covered in 12th-century biblical reliefs and a Mantegna altarpiece inside. The surrounding San Zeno neighborhood is quieter and noticeably more residential — worth a wander.

San Zeno · outskirts

Biblioteca Capitolare

One of the oldest continuously operating libraries in the world, quietly tucked beside the Duomo. Manuscripts here predate Verona's more famous sights by centuries — a five-minute stop most visitors walk straight past.

Valpolicella hills

The vineyard-covered hills where Amarone and Valpolicella wine are made start just 15–20 minutes outside the city — rolling countryside, family-run cantinas, and views back toward Verona that most guests never see.

Where to eat

Real Veronese cooking — the kind locals actually order, from tiny family-run osterias to a spot with a proper vegetarian, vegan, and gluten-free menu. Veg-friendly options are flagged below.

Bistro con Amore Vini e Cucina

Only a corridor wide, with chef Cristian cooking in full view of the room — and singing along to opera while he does it. The menu changes with what's fresh, but the eggplant, the veal liver with polenta, and a tiramisu regulars call one of the best in the city are the things to order.

City center
View on map

L'Evangelista Ristorante & Enoteca

A block from Piazza Bra, with an actual wine list to match the "enoteca" in the name and full vegan, vegetarian, and gluten-free menus alongside the regular one — rare in a restaurant this serious about traditional Veneto cooking. Outdoor seating on the quiet alley out front.

Il Vicoletto Trattoria

Five minutes from the Arena but worlds away from the crowds — a small, well-kept trattoria doing proper Veronese cooking: bigoli with duck ragù, potato gnocchi with pastissada de caval. Seating is tight, so book ahead.

Near the Arena
View on map

Osteria Sottoriva

Set right into the medieval arcade it's named for, with an honest-to-goodness ancient wine cellar underneath. Cheap, unpretentious, and exactly the kind of place locals mean when they warn you off the restaurants around Piazza Bra — go for the salami board and, if you're feeling bold, the horse stew.

Via Sottoriva
View on map

Osteria Il Bertoldo

A tiny, twenty-seat spot tucked off a side street near Piazza dei Signori — exposed brick, low light, and a short, seasonal menu that leans into Veronese ingredients with a slightly more creative touch.

Near Piazza dei Signori
View on map

Trattoria Alla Colonna

Open since 1985, and famous for one thing: the cotoletta, a breaded veal cutlet the size of a dinner plate, born by accident when a substitute cook served one differently than usual and it stuck. Old-school, unfussy, and exactly what a Veronese trattoria should be.

Since 1985
View on map

Café Carducci

Hidden away in the university quarter of Veronetta since 1928, with white tablecloths, a mosaic tile floor, and ornate red brick that makes it feel like stepping back in time. A quieter, more elegant option across the river from the main tourist crush.

Where to drink

Verona sits at the edge of Amarone and Valpolicella country, so the wine list here is serious — plus a couple of cocktail spots if you're in the mood for something else.

Enoteca Segreta

True to its name — tucked down a dead-end alley near Juliet's house, with just four tables set in an actual wine cellar. Candlelit, intimate, and the kind of place you'd walk right past if you didn't know to look for it.

Osteria del Bugiardo

The historic tasting room of Cantina Buglioni, right in the center of town — big communal wooden tables, excellent cutting boards, and local wine at prices that don't punish you for being three minutes from Piazza delle Erbe.

Tor-tor

Just behind the Arena, run by owners who built the wine list entirely around what they actually like to drink — not what tourists expect to see. Laid-back, unpretentious, and a good reset after a day of sightseeing.

Enoteca La Veronese

Right across from Ponte Pietra, where sommelier Cesare will ask what mood you're in before he pours anything — and actually mean it. Come for one glass, leave having learned the whole story behind it.
