City center & a little further out

Eat, wander, and drink like you live here.

Verona rewards a slower pace. The postcard sights are worth it, but the best meals and quietest corners are usually one street back from where everyone else is standing. Four lists, organized so you can find what you're after.

The essentials

Yes, everyone goes here — and yes, they're worth it. These are Verona's must-sees, the ones you'll recognize before you've even landed.

Verona Arena

A first-century Roman amphitheater that still hosts opera under the stars every summer — one of the best-preserved in the world. Even without a ticket, walking the same stone tiers as ancient Veronese crowds is worth the detour.

Piazza Bra

Casa di Giulietta

The (almost certainly fictional) balcony that launched a thousand photos. Shakespeare never set foot in Verona, and the house wasn't linked to the Capulets until the 20th century — but the courtyard, covered in love notes, is a genuinely fun stop.

Via Cappello
View on map

Castelvecchio & Ponte Scaligero

A red-brick medieval fortress turned museum, with its own crenellated bridge stretching across the Adige. Skip the entrance queue and just walk the bridge at golden hour — one of the prettiest views in the city, free of charge.

Corso Castelvecchio
View on map

Piazza delle Erbe

The old Roman forum, now Verona's liveliest square — market stalls by day, aperitivo crowds by evening, all wrapped in frescoed facades. Come once for the sightseeing, come back for the people-watching.

City center
View on map

Torre dei Lamberti

Climb (or take the lift, no judgment) 84 meters above Piazza delle Erbe for the best rooftop view in Verona — pink terracotta roofs in every direction, the Adige curling past, and the Alps on a clear day.

84m up

Ponte Pietra

Verona's oldest bridge — Roman-built, partially rebuilt after WWII with the original stones fished back out of the river. Cross it at sunset for the classic postcard view back toward the historic center.

Verona Cathedral

A Romanesque-Gothic cathedral with a Titian altarpiece tucked inside — quieter and less crowded than the Arena or Juliet's House, and a nice five-minute stop if you're already walking the river.

Off the well-trodden path

The spots locals actually go — some tucked into the historic center, others a short walk across the river or just outside the walls. Worth the extra ten minutes.

Giardino Giusti

A 16th-century Renaissance garden behind an unmarked door in Veronetta. Follow the cypress allée to the back and climb the narrow stone stairway inside the hillside tower for a secret, mostly-empty view over the entire city.

Veronetta · outskirts
View on map

Castel San Pietro & Teatro Romano

Ride the little funicular up from the Roman theater — still used for summer performances — to a hilltop fortress with arguably a better skyline view than Torre dei Lamberti, and a fraction of the crowd.

Veronetta · outskirts
View on map

Basilica di San Zeno Maggiore

One of Italy's finest Romanesque churches, with a bronze door covered in 12th-century biblical reliefs and a Mantegna altarpiece inside. The surrounding San Zeno neighborhood is quieter and noticeably more residential — worth a wander.

San Zeno · outskirts
View on map

Biblioteca Capitolare

One of the oldest continuously operating libraries in the world, quietly tucked beside the Duomo. Manuscripts here predate Verona's more famous sights by centuries — a five-minute stop most visitors walk straight past.

Sottoriva

A covered medieval portico running along the old town between Ponte Nuovo and the church of Sant'Anastasia — low stone arches, quiet in the afternoon, and a favorite backstreet of locals cutting across the center.

Valpolicella hills

The vineyard-covered hills where Amarone and Valpolicella wine are made start just 15–20 minutes outside the city — rolling countryside, family-run cantinas, and views back toward Verona that most guests never see.

Where to eat

Real Veronese cooking — the kind locals actually order — plus a few dedicated vegetarian and vegan spots. Veg-friendly options are flagged below.

Ristorante

Osteria al Duca

A no-frills, old-school osteria inside a 13th-century building once linked to Romeo's real-life family, the Montecchi. Go for classic Veronese plates — risotto all'Amarone is the safe, excellent bet.

Historic center

Ristorante

Trattoria al Pompiere

Running since 1907, with green checkered tablecloths and walls lined in black-and-white photos. Known for one of the best cheese and salumi selections in the city, alongside solid, unfussy Veronese classics.

Osteria Il Bertoldo

Where to drink

Verona sits at the edge of Amarone and Valpolicella country, so the wine list here is serious — plus a few cocktail spots if you're in the mood for something else.

Enoteca

Antica Bottega del Vino

One of Verona's oldest wine bars, dating to the 16th century as the "Osteria lo Scudo di Francia." Centuries-old wood-beamed rooms and an enormous list heavy on Amarone and Valpolicella — the closest thing to a living wine museum in the city.

Dal Zovo Wine Bar

Family-run since 1958 and now on its third generation. Order a flight of Valpolicella or a glass of good-value Champagne, paired with a simple plate of focaccia — unpretentious and exactly what a neighborhood wine bar should be.
